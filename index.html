International Conference 2025

Data ethics for historical research in a digital era

Critical reflections and solutions

Mainz,10-12 November 2025

About

Nowadays, the use of data, Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools and data science techniques are becoming increasingly pervasive in daily life and research activities attracting many scholars to use them in historically oriented research with promising results. But neither data nor computer science techniques are neutral, amplifying possible gender and cultural biases and under-representation of certain cultures or minorities due to unequal access to infrastructures and inherent data biases. These issues increase when technologies are applied to historical datasets and archives, which are the result of a selection of the available information, driven by the power dynamics and cultural and gender biases of the collectors.

Therefore, ethical aspects arise in the life-cycle of data, from their creation to their dissemination, to guarantee a fair, equal and non- discriminatory representation of cultures and a respectful treatment of sensitive data. These challenges create the need for a critical reflection on the ethical implications of the use of data and technologies and for solutions to be adopted.

Such topics are crucial and widely addressed by the community of data and computer scientists, as the developments of data ethics, ethical AI, and the data colonialism critique highlight. Nevertheless, the topic has been scarcely and partially addressed by the historical research domain, making a common reflection about the issues declined in specific needs and characteristics of the sector necessary. In this context, a common effort towards the seeking, definition and adoption of shared guidelines and solutions is particularly urgent.

This conference aims at focusing on what the ethical dimensions of data usage means in the domain of historical research practice, fostering discussions among scholars and providing practical tools and strategies to tackle these issues.
In particular, the objectives of the conference are the followings:

  • raising awareness in historical research of the ethical components of data use and data-driven research through critically addressing the prevalence of Western methods, data coverage and infrastructures in the field
  • fostering dialogue among researchers who encounter ethical issues in their work
  • increasing knowledge of strategies and best practices for ethical data use and enable participants to share their experiences, strategies and tools to address ethical issues in historical research.

To allow a worldwide participation, the language of the conference will be English. The conference will be an hybrid event, although the physical attendance of presenters will be preferred.

Call for proposals

Themes and topics

The conference aims to cover a range of themes and topics related to data ethics, unequal access to IT infrastructures in historical research, and representations of power, in the broad sense of every kind of historically-oriented discipline (e.g., history of science, archeology, history of art, and so on). We therefore invite researchers to submit papers on, but not limited to, the following topics, applied to historical research scenarios:

  • Sensitive data: Addressing ethical issues related to sensitive data, such as personal and health data (from a historical perspective) or data on human remains. This may include discussions of oral history data collection and the ethical issues it raises.
  • Discriminatory language in databases: Exploring the issues and implications of discriminatory (e.g. racist and racialising) language in databases, and discussing existing practices to address it. This theme also links historical research with the work of practitioners in GLAM institutions on the datasets and classification systems of their collections.
  • AI ethics: Raising awareness of ethical uses of AI tools in historical research, also with a focus on EU and global regulations. Highlighting the challenges historians face when working with these tools and the ethical issues they raise. exploring how biases in AI training data affect historical research.
  • Data storage and infrastructure: Examining the power structures inherent in data storage and infrastructure, including a post-colonial perspective and questions of data sovereignty, issues of access, control and isolation.
  • Unequal representation of culture, gender, and communities: Discussing who is represented in data sets and shedding light on links between underrepresentation and power, including gender diversity, data feminism and related topics.
  • Environmental aspects: Considering the environmental impact of data storage and discussing the sustainable use of computational methods.
  • Misused data: Addressing the exploitation of data to spread misinformation and its implications for historical research

To foster the sharing of solutions and good practices, contributions that propose a concrete solution for the ethical issues encountered during the research are particularly encouraged.
Although the conference is centered on historical research, proposals coming from other disciplines are also welcomed, to foster a cross-domain dissemination of common reflections and strategies.

Call for proposals

We invite researchers to submit proposals, written in English, on, but not limited to, the above topics, in the form of abstracts of 500 words. We welcome contributions from scholars at all career levels, especially (but not exclusively) from the field of any kind of historically orientated discipline (e.g. history of science, archeology, art history, etc.) or researcher from the computer science, digital humanities fields (and related) dealing with research applied to historical disciplines. Two types of submissions are welcomed:

  • Presentations: a 20 minutes presentation, plus time for questions (10 minutes)
  • Hands-on sessions: participants submit proposals for hand-on sessions that shows how to tackle the ethic-related topics when applied to practical historical research as outlined above. The application should include the desired length of the session and further material support needed. The maximum length of each session is 90 minutes. The sessions should be designed in a way that prior knowledge of the topic is not required.

The proposals will be evaluated through a peer-review process.

Travel funding

We are currently working to provide travel funding to speakers in order to support global participation, including speakers from the global south and indigenous people. During the application phase, it will be possible to express the interest in participating into the funding program.
To apply to the travel funding program, please specify the location you will be traveling from as well as, the expected cost of your travel, in your application.

Conference Proceedings

The proceedings of the conference will be published in an edited, open access e-book as part of the IEG publications series. Therefore, the authors of papers presented in the conference will be invited to extend the proposal in a full paper (maximum 9000 words, including footnotes, bibliography excluded). The submitted full papers will be further selected through a peer-review. Further details will be provided after the conference.
A Microsoft Word template and further indications about the bibliographic citation format will be provided.
